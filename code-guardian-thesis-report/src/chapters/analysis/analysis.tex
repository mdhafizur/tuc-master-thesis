This chapter defines the requirements for automated vulnerability detection and repair assistance in an IDE and positions the thesis approach against related work. The focus is privacy-preserving local LLM support with retrieval grounding and practical developer usability.


\section{Requirements}
\label{sec:requirements}

The thesis targets local IDE assistance for JavaScript/TypeScript development: detect vulnerabilities, explain them, and propose repairs without code exfiltration. Compared with rule-based SAST, the approach adds LLM reasoning and optional retrieval grounding, but must still satisfy strict operational constraints.

Based on the problem statement and related work, six requirements define the evaluation baseline:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{R1 -- Detection accuracy and consistency:} reliable findings with stable behavior and controlled false positives.
  \item \textbf{R2 -- Context-aware reasoning:} distinguish exploitable patterns from benign code using surrounding program context.
  \item \textbf{R3 -- Explainability and transparency:} provide clear, technically precise, and traceable explanations.
  \item \textbf{R4 -- Actionable repairs:} suggest minimal, security-improving fixes while preserving developer control.
  \item \textbf{R5 -- Privacy-preserving operation:} keep analysis and retrieval local, with no source-code transmission.
  \item \textbf{R6 -- Usability and responsiveness:} deliver feedback at latencies compatible with IDE workflows.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Critical Comparison Across Paradigms}
\label{sec:related-work-critical-comparison}

The reviewed literature indicates that security assistants should be compared as \emph{design paradigms}, not as isolated tools. Table~\ref{tab:related-work-paradigm-compare} summarizes the trade-offs that motivate this thesis architecture.

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{Critical comparison of major security-assistance paradigms.}
  \label{tab:related-work-paradigm-compare}
  \footnotesize
  \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.15}
  \begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{p{0.18\textwidth}XXX}
    \toprule
    Paradigm & Primary strengths & Primary limitations & Implication for this thesis \\
    \midrule
    Rule-based SAST & Deterministic behavior, reproducible CI/CD integration, explicit audit trails. & Limited semantic adaptation and weak repair guidance. & Keep deterministic baseline signals and add contextual reasoning. \\
    LLM-only IDE assistant & Flexible reasoning, explanation, and repair suggestion in context. & Probabilistic behavior, hallucination risk, and variable false-positive control. & Enforce strict output contracts and developer-controlled patching. \\
    RAG-assisted LLM & Better grounding and explanation alignment in favorable setups. & Model-dependent gains and additional latency/complexity. & Treat RAG as tunable per model/workflow, not universal. \\
    Local-first deployment & Strong source-code privacy boundary and reproducibility. & Dependence on local hardware/host security assumptions. & Prioritize no-exfiltration while documenting residual risks. \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabularx}
\end{table}

Two conclusions follow. First, no single paradigm satisfies R1--R6 alone; practical systems combine complementary mechanisms. Second, evaluation must jointly report quality and operational metrics because deployment value depends on recall, false-positive control, robustness, latency, and privacy guarantees together.
